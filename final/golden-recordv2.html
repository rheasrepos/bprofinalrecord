The ΛCDM Cosmological Model

The ΛCDM Cosmological Model is our best description of the universe at its largest scales. It comprises a nearly flat spacetime expanding under the influence of cold dark matter and a constant vacuum energy density (Λ) that currently dominates and drives accelerating expansion.

Composition of the Universe

Dark Energy (Λ) 68%

Vacuum energy driving accelerating expansion. Nature unknown.

Dark Matter (CDM) 27%

Does not interact with light. Detected only gravitationally.

Ordinary/Baryonic Matter 5%

Atoms. Every star, planet, and living thing ever observed.

The Friedmann Equations

Derived from Einstein's theory of general relativity, these equations describe how a homogeneous, isotropic universe expands over time.

Friedmann I — Expansion Rate
H² = (ȧ/a)² = (8πG/3)ρ − kc²/a² + Λc²/3
H = Hubble parameter | a = scale factor | ρ = energy density | k = spatial curvature (≈0, flat) | Λ = cosmological constant
Friedmann II — Acceleration
ä/a = −(4πG/3)(ρ + 3p/c²) + Λc²/3
ä > 0 means accelerating expansion. The Λ term currently dominates, confirmed by Type Ia supernova observations (Nobel Prize 2011).
The universe is expanding at an accelerating rate. Everything beyond our local group of galaxies is receding faster with every passing moment. On cosmic timescales, distant galaxies will eventually cross beyond our observational horizon entirely.

Key Evidence

Galaxy Redshifts

All distant galaxies are receding from us at an accelerating rate; the further away the galaxy, the faster its recession. Space itself is isotropically expanding in all directions.

Type Ia Supernovae

Standard candles are fainter than expected fpr a static or decelerating universe. The universe is thus accelerating.

CMB

The thermal afterglow of the early universe is measured at 2.725 K. Tiny fluctuations in this background radiation match ΛCDM predictions precisely.

Rotation Curves

Galactic stars orbit too fast for gravitation from visible matter alone. Massive amounts of dark matter are required to explain these rotation curves.

The Standard Model of Particle Physics

The Standard Model is a quantum field theory describing all known fundamental particles and three of the four fundamental forces (electromagnetic, weak nuclear, strong nuclear). Gravity is not yet incorporated. Despite this, it is one of the most precisely tested theories in science, with strong empirical evidence coming from measurements and discoveries such as that of the Higgs boson at CERN's Large Hadron Collider in 2012.

Standard Model Particle Table
■ Quarks ■ Leptons ■ Bosons ■ Higgs

The Standard Model is yet to be mathematically reconciled with the general relativity foundations of the macro-scale ΛCDM cosmological model — it does not explain dark matter, dark energy, or gravity at quantum scales. A unified theory remains the central unsolved problem of physics.

Galaxies & Large-Scale Structure

Galaxies are gravitationally bound systems of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter. At the largest scales they form a vast cosmic web of filaments, sheets, and voids shaped by gravity over billions of years.

Galaxy Morphologies

Galaxies are broadly classified into several morphological types. Elliptical galaxies are smooth, rounded systems dominated by older stars. Spiral galaxies contain rotating disks with spiral arms where active star formation occurs. Lenticular galaxies are intermediate systems with a disk but little gas or spiral structure. Irregular galaxies lack a defined shape and are often shaped by gravitational interactions or mergers.

Large-Scale Structure

On the largest observable scales, galaxies trace out a cosmic web composed of filaments, sheets, and vast empty voids. These structures formed as tiny density fluctuations in the early universe grew under gravity over 13.8 billion years.

Stars — Cosmic Forges

Stars are gravitationally bound nuclear fusion reactors. They are the factories that produce every element heavier than hydrogen and helium, and they distribute those elements through the universe in the various, sometimes spectacular means in which they die.

The Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram

Organises stars by luminosity (vertical) and surface temperature (horizontal). The diagonal main sequence is where stars spend most of their lives fusing hydrogen.

Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram

Stellar Lifecycle

Nebula / Molecular Cloud
Gravity causes a cloud of gas and dust to collapse. As it contracts, it heats up.
Protostar
A dense core forms. Temperatures rise until hydrogen fusion ignites.
Main Sequence Star
Fuses hydrogen into helium for millions to billions of years. Our Sun is here now.
Red Giant / Supergiant
Hydrogen exhausted. Core contracts; outer layers expand. Fusion of heavier elements begins.
Death: Supernova / Planetary Nebula
Massive stars explode as supernovae, seeding space with heavy elements. Smaller stars shed outer layers gently.
Remnant: White Dwarf / Neutron Star / Black Hole
The stellar core remains. Mass determines the endpoint: white dwarf, neutron star, or black hole.

We are made of stardust — literally. Every element in our bodies heavier than hydrogen was forged in a stellar core and ejected in a stellar death.

Solar System & Planet Formation

Our Solar System formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from the gravitational collapse of a cloud of gas and dust. A central star ignited; the remaining disc of material accreted into planets, moons, asteroids, and comets over tens of millions of years.

Our Solar System
Rocky Terrestrial Planets

Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars. Dense iron cores, silicate mantles, thin or no atmospheres (except Venus/Earth).

Asteroid Belt

Between Mars and Jupiter. Rocky and metallic bodies — material that never coalesced into a planet due to Jupiter's gravitational influence.

Gas Giants

Jupiter and Saturn. Massive hydrogen-helium envelopes. No solid surface. Jupiter's mass exceeds all other planets combined.

Ice Giants & Kuiper Belt

Uranus and Neptune. Water, methane, ammonia ices beneath hydrogen atmospheres. Beyond: the Kuiper belt, Pluto, and the Oort cloud.

Evolution & Tree of Life

All life on Earth descended from a single common ancestor (LUCA — Last Universal Common Ancestor) approximately 3.8 billion years ago. Four billion years of evolution by natural selection has produced the extraordinary diversity of life we observe today.

Every organism alive — from archaea in hydrothermal vents to blue whales to the oak tree outside a window — is your cousin. The tree of life is the most comprehensive genealogy ever traced.

Human Anatomy

We are bilaterally symmetrical, warm-blooded, oxygen-breathing vertebrates. Average mass ~70 kg. Bipedal locomotion. Greatly enlarged cerebral cortex. Lifespan 70–90 years.

Skeletal System

206 bones. Endoskeleton of calcium phosphate. Provides structure, protects organs, enables movement.

Nervous System

~86 billion neurons. Central (brain + spinal cord) and peripheral. Electrochemical signalling at ~120 m/s.

Cardiovascular System

Four-chambered heart. ~5 litres of blood. Closed circulatory system pumping ~7,000 litres/day.

Genome

~3 billion base pairs. ~20,000 protein-coding genes. 99.9% identical between any two humans.
