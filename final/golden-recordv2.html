The ΛCDM Cosmological Model

The ΛCDM Cosmological Model is our best description of the universe at its largest scales. It comprises a nearly flat spacetime expanding under the influence of cold dark matter and a constant vacuum energy density (Λ) that currently dominates and drives accelerating expansion.

Composition of the Universe

Dark Energy (Λ) 68%

Vacuum energy driving accelerating expansion. Nature unknown.

Dark Matter (CDM) 27%

Does not interact with light. Detected only gravitationally.

Ordinary/Baryonic Matter 5%

Atoms. Every star, planet, and living thing ever observed.

The Friedmann Equations

Derived from Einstein's theory of general relativity, these equations describe how a homogeneous, isotropic universe expands over time.

Friedmann I — Expansion Rate
H² = (ȧ/a)² = (8πG/3)ρ − kc²/a² + Λc²/3
H = Hubble parameter | a = scale factor | ρ = energy density | k = spatial curvature (≈0, flat) | Λ = cosmological constant
Friedmann II — Acceleration
ä/a = −(4πG/3)(ρ + 3p/c²) + Λc²/3
ä > 0 means accelerating expansion. The Λ term currently dominates, confirmed by Type Ia supernova observations (Nobel Prize 2011).
The universe is expanding at an accelerating rate. Everything beyond our local group of galaxies is receding faster with every passing moment. On cosmic timescales, distant galaxies will eventually cross beyond our observational horizon entirely.

Key Evidence

Galaxy Redshifts

All distant galaxies are receding from us at an accelerating rate; the further away the galaxy, the faster its recession. Space itself is isotropically expanding in all directions.

Type Ia Supernovae

Standard candles are fainter than expected fpr a static or decelerating universe. The universe is thus accelerating.

CMB

The thermal afterglow of the early universe is measured at 2.725 K. Tiny fluctuations in this background radiation match ΛCDM predictions precisely.

Rotation Curves

Galactic stars orbit too fast for gravitation from visible matter alone. Massive amounts of dark matter are required to explain these rotation curves.

The Standard Model of Particle Physics

The Standard Model is a quantum field theory describing all known fundamental particles and three of the four fundamental forces (electromagnetic, weak nuclear, strong nuclear). Gravity is not yet incorporated. Despite this, it is one of the most precisely tested theories in science, with strong empirical evidence coming from measurements and discoveries such as that of the Higgs boson at CERN's Large Hadron Collider in 2012.

Standard Model Particle Table
■ Quarks ■ Leptons ■ Bosons ■ Higgs

The Standard Model is yet to be mathematically reconciled with the general relativity foundations of the macro-scale ΛCDM cosmological model — it does not explain dark matter, dark energy, or gravity at quantum scales. A unified theory remains the central unsolved problem of physics.

Galaxies & Large-Scale Structure

Galaxies are gravitationally bound systems of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter, often with smaller galaxies (dwarf galaxies) orbiting/infalling onto larger ones.

Galaxy Morphologies

Galaxies are broadly classified into several morphological types. Elliptical galaxies are smooth, rounded systems dominated by older stars. Spiral galaxies contain rotating disks with spiral arms where active star formation occurs. Lenticular galaxies are intermediate systems with a disk but little gas or spiral structure. Irregular galaxies lack a defined shape and are often results of gravitational interactions or mergers.

Large-Scale Structure

On the largest observable scales, galaxies trace out a cosmic web composed of filaments, sheets, and vast empty voids. These structures formed as tiny density fluctuations in the early universe and grew under gravity over 13+ billion years.

Cosmic Web Simulation

Cosmological simulation visualizing the formation of the large-scale structure of the universe, showing galaxy filaments, clusters, and voids emerging over time.

Stars — Cosmic Forges

Stars are gravitationally bound nuclear fusion reactors. They are the factories that produce every element heavier than hydrogen and helium, and they distribute those elements through the universe in the various, sometimes spectacular means in which they die.

The Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram

The Hertzprung-Russell Diagram organises stars by luminosity (vertical) and surface temperature (horizontal). The diagonal main sequence is where stars spend most of their lives fusing hydrogen, while the red giant branch and white dwarf cluster represent different stages in a star's life.

Stellar Lifecycle

Stellar Evolution — by Mass

Nebula / Molecular Cloud

A dense core forms. Temperatures rise until hydrogen fusion ignites.

Remnant: White Dwarf / Neutron Star / Black Hole
The stellar core remains. Mass determines the endpoint: white dwarf, neutron star, or black hole.

Every element in our bodies heavier than hydrogen was forged in a stellar core and ejected in a stellar death. We, along with everything else in our universe, are quite literally made of stardust.

Solar System & Planet Formation

Our Solar System formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from the gravitational collapse of a cloud of gas and dust. A central star ignited while the remaining disc of material accreted into planets, moons, asteroids, and comets over tens of millions of years.

Our Solar System
Rocky Terrestrial Planets

Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars. These planets have dense iron cores, silicate mantles, thin or no atmospheres (except Venus/Earth).

Asteroid Belt

Covering the expanse between Mars and Jupiter. This comprises of rocky and metallic bodies and are leftover material that never coalesced into a planet due to Jupiter's gravitational influence.

Gas Giants

Jupiter and Saturn. These planets have massive hydrogen-helium envelopes but no solid surface. Jupiter's mass exceeds all other planets combined.

Ice Giants & Kuiper Belt

Uranus and Neptune. These planets are made of water, methane, ammonia ices beneath hydrogen atmospheres. Beyond them is the Kuiper belt, Pluto, and the Oort cloud.

Evolution & Tree of Life

All life on Earth descended from a single common ancestor (LUCA — Last Universal Common Ancestor) approximately 3.8 billion years ago. Since then, billion years of evolution by natural selection has produced the extraordinary diversity of life we observe today.

Technosignatures

A civilisation at our stage of development leaves detectable traces in the electromagnetic spectrum, the atmosphere, and in orbit. These technosignatures — observable signs of technological activity — are what a distant observer might detect before ever intercepting a deliberate signal.

Radio Signals

4G LTE cell towers, Wi-Fi networks, GPS satellites, and planetary radar installations continuously emit radio waves across a broad range of frequencies. Earth has been a detectable radio source since the early 20th century. This radio bubble, now over 100 light-years in radius, is the furthest-reaching evidence of our existence.

Optical Signals

High-powered lasers, developed for communications research and directed-energy applications, produce coherent light capable of standing out against the Sun's background radiation across interstellar distances. Deliberate laser pulses, if aimed, could be detectable by a civilisation scanning for narrowband optical emission.

Atmospheric & Surface Impacts

Industrial nitrogen dioxide (NO₂) concentrations in Earth's atmosphere have no plausible natural source at observed levels. Thermal and optical imaging of the night side of Earth reveals city lights concentrated along coastlines and river systems — patterns inconsistent with any geological process. These are unambiguous indicators of a technological civilisation.

Artificial Objects

Earth is surrounded by thousands of active satellites, defunct spacecraft, and debris in stable orbits. Lunar landing modules remain on the Moon's surface. The Voyager and Pioneer probes are on trajectories leaving the solar system entirely. These objects constitute a distributed, persistent record of a spacefaring species — artefacts that will outlast any signal we transmit.
