Drake Equation Looper

The New Golden Record · Course Final Project · BPRO28800 / BIOS13142 / ASTR18700
The Drake Equation, proposed by Frank Drake in 1961, estimates N: the number of communicating civilizations in the Milky Way. It is a product of seven variables spanning astrophysics, planetary science, biology, and sociology. The equation was never intended to produce a definitive answer — its purpose is to structure scientific uncertainty and identify which variables most constrain N (Drake et al., as discussed in Lecture 1, Sereno). In this instrument, each variable is assigned a synthesizer voice. The knob sets the estimated value for that variable; activating the pad incorporates it into the running product. The sound properties of each voice — its timing, density, pitch, and behavior — are designed to reflect the scientific character of the variable it represents. Relevant readings from the course syllabus are cited for each variable below.
N = R★ · fₚ · nₑ · fₗ · fᵢ · fᶜ · L
Modulation
The modulation section sweeps a selected variable's value automatically over time using a low-frequency oscillator (LFO). Scientific justification: several Drake Equation parameters are not static — they vary over cosmic timescales. R★ has declined since the peak of cosmic star formation (~10 billion years ago). fₗ and fᵢ may be time-dependent if life and intelligence take billions of years to emerge. L is particularly dynamic if civilizations undergo boom-collapse cycles. The modulation feature allows the listener to hear how a continuously varying parameter changes both the N estimate and the resulting sound texture, modeling the non-stationarity of these variables. LFO shapes (sine, triangle, sawtooth) represent different drift behaviors: gradual oscillation, linear ramp, or asymmetric change.
