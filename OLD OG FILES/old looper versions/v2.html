Cosmology & Lambda CDM

Our most successful cosmological model is a universe comprising a nearly flat spacetime whose expansion rate is determined by cold gravitating matter and a constant vacuum energy density (\(\Lambda\)).

The Geometry of Spacetime

Observations reveal that space itself changes with time. Distances between unbound objects grow according to a scale factor \(a(t)\). The geometry is described by a homogeneous, isotropic metric:

$$ ds^2 = -c^2 dt^2 + a^2(t) d\Sigma_k^2 $$

Expansion Governed by Energy Content

Spacetime curvature responds to energy density and pressure. The expansion rate depends on total energy density (\(\rho\)), spatial curvature (\(k\)), and a constant vacuum term (\(\Lambda\)).

$$ H^2 = \left(\frac{\dot a}{a}\right)^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho - \frac{k c^2}{a^2} + \frac{\Lambda c^2}{3} $$

Exoplanetary Physics & Habitability

Planet mass determines composition, atmosphere retention, internal structure, and geological activity, making it the primary driver of planetary diversity.

Atmospheric Retention & Escape Velocity

Higher mass means a higher escape velocity, allowing a planet to retain light gases like Hydrogen and Helium. If mass is too low, the atmosphere escapes over time. If it is too high, it becomes a thick hydrogen envelope with no rocky surface.

$$ v_{esc} = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}} $$

Thermal & Geological Evolution

Planetary heat sources include accretional heating, radioactive decay, core formation, and tidal heating. This internal heat dictates whether plate tectonics can operate and if magnetic fields form. Earth's protective dynamo requires a liquid metallic core and sufficient mass to maintain convection.

Planetary Formation & Climate

  • Classical Accretion: The early solar nebula saw dust grains clump into mountain-sized planetesimals, merging into embryos, and undergoing chaotic impacts to form terrestrial planets [Chambers, 2004].
  • Pebble Accretion: Modern observations of faraway protoplanetary disks suggest planets formed much faster as millimeter-to-meter-sized pebbles drifted inward and were swept up by planetary cores [Boyle, 2022].
  • The Thermostat: Earth maintained a habitable climate and liquid water over billions of years due to the carbonate-silicate geochemical cycle regulating atmospheric CO2 [Kasting et al., 1988].

Evolutionary History & Biosignatures

  • The Human Bottleneck: Genomic evidence reveals human ancestors faced a near-extinction population bottleneck roughly 900,000 years ago during the Pleistocene transition, severely restricting and shaping modern genetic diversity [Hu et al., 2023].
  • The Most Invasive Species: Homo sapiens dominated the globe through a unique evolutionary combination of "hyper-prosociality" and advanced projectile weapons, allowing us to outcompete other hominids [Marean, 2015].
  • Disequilibrium: The simultaneous presence of highly reactive gases, such as abundant oxygen coexisting with methane, serves as a robust biosignature since they require a continuous biological source to replenish them [Schwieterman & Leung, 2024].

Artificial Intelligence in Science

  • The Automation Paradox: AI tools drastically increase the output and impact of individual scientists, but they simultaneously restrict the collective focus of the scientific field. AI-augmented research tends to gravitate toward established, data-rich areas, automating existing paradigms rather than exploring completely novel ones [Hao et al., forthcoming].
  • Human-Aware Systems: For AI to meaningfully accelerate scientific discovery, it must be designed as "human-aware" to understand human hypotheses. These systems can target our collective blind spots and suggest the novel, unexplored connections we naturally miss [Sourati & Evans, 2023].
  • The Benefit of Bias: Perfectly neutral AI systems induce "automation bias," causing humans to uncritically accept the AI's output. Introducing culturally or politically biased AI actually improves collective human decision-making by provoking skepticism and forcing users to critically engage with the information [Lai et al., 2025].
