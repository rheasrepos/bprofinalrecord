Context

Why a New Record?

In 1977, Carl Sagan and his team curated a golden phonograph record aboard the Voyager spacecrafts — a 12-inch, gold-plated copper disc containing sounds and images chosen to portray life and culture on Earth, meant for any intelligent extraterrestrial life that might one day encounter it.

Almost 50 years later, the original record reflects its era: optimistic, western-centric, scientifically limited by what we then knew about the cosmos, and silent on almost everything that has happened since.

This project is a reimagining. What would we include if we were sending the record today? What science has transformed our understanding? Whose voices were missing? What truths about humanity — beautiful and difficult — deserve a place in our cosmic autobiography?

— Ha Do & Rhea Madhogarhia

Mathematics &
Physics

The Standard Model

The quantum field theory describing all known fundamental particles and three of the four fundamental forces (electromagnetism, weak nuclear, strong nuclear) via the symmetry group:

$$ SU(3)_C \times SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y $$

Matter particles (fermions) come in three generations of quarks and leptons. Force carriers (gauge bosons) include 8 gluons, the W± and Z bosons, and the photon. The Higgs boson (discovered at CERN in 2012) completes the picture, giving mass to particles via spontaneous symmetry breaking.

Key Constants & Equations

  • Newton's Law of Gravitation\(F = Gm_1m_2/r^2\) — the universal inverse-square force between masses.
  • General Relativity (Einstein Field Equations)\(G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G T_{\mu\nu}/c^4\)
  • Special Relativity\(E = mc^2\) and the Lorentz factor \(\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}\)
  • Schrödinger Equation\(i\hbar \partial_t |\psi\rangle = \hat{H}|\psi\rangle\) — the fundamental equation of quantum mechanics.
  • Gravitational WavesRipples in spacetime first directly detected by LIGO in 2015 — new ears for the universe.
  • Powers of TenScale from 10⁻¹⁵ m (nucleus) to 10²⁶ m (observable universe), demonstrating cosmic scale.

The Universe:
ΛCDM

The Geometry of Spacetime

Our most successful cosmological model describes a universe whose expansion rate is determined by cold gravitating matter and a constant vacuum energy density (Λ), with the latter currently dominating and causing accelerated expansion.

Observations reveal that space itself changes with time. Distances between unbound objects grow according to a scale factor a(t), described by a homogeneous, isotropic metric:

$$ ds^2 = -c^2 dt^2 + a^2(t)\, d\Sigma_k^2 $$

The Friedmann Equation

Spacetime curvature responds to energy density and pressure. The expansion rate depends on total energy density (ρ), spatial curvature (k), and the cosmological constant (Λ):

$$ H^2 = \left(\frac{\dot a}{a}\right)^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho - \frac{k c^2}{a^2} + \frac{\Lambda c^2}{3} $$

Current observations: Ω_m ≈ 0.3, Ω_Λ ≈ 0.7, Ω_k ≈ 0. The universe is nearly flat and vacuum-energy dominated.

Galaxies, Stars &
Nucleosynthesis

Types of Galaxies

  • Spiral Galaxies (e.g., The Milky Way)Central bulge, rotating disk, spiral arms, dark matter halo. Ongoing star formation; blue young stars in arms. Gas-rich environments.
  • Elliptical Galaxies (e.g., Messier 87)Smooth, rounded shape. Mostly old red stars; little gas or dust; very little new star formation.
  • Irregular Galaxies (e.g., Large Magellanic Cloud)No clear symmetry; often distorted by gravitational interactions with neighboring galaxies.

Stellar Evolution by Mass

A star's mass at birth determines everything: how hot it burns, how long it lives, how it dies, and what elements it creates.

  • Low-mass stars (<0.5 M☉)Burn hydrogen slowly for trillions of years. Never ignite helium. End as helium white dwarfs. No heavy element production.
  • Sun-like stars (0.5–8 M☉)Main sequence → Red Giant → Helium burning (triple-alpha: 3 ⁴He → ¹²C) → Planetary nebula → Carbon-oxygen white dwarf. s-process creates elements like Strontium, Barium.
  • Massive stars (>8 M☉)Onion-shell fusion structure up to iron. When the iron core exceeds the Chandrasekhar limit (~1.4 M☉), it collapses. Core-collapse supernova. r-process creates gold, uranium, platinum, rare earths.
$$ \text{Stellar Lifetime} \propto M^{-2.5} $$

All elements heavier than helium originate from stellar nucleosynthesis. We are literally made of dead stars.

Exoplanets &
Habitability

Mass as the Master Variable

Just as stellar mass determines a star's entire life story, planetary mass determines composition, atmosphere retention, internal structure, geological activity, and long-term evolution.

$$ v_{esc} = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}} $$

Higher mass → higher escape velocity → better retention of light gases (H, He). Too low: atmosphere escapes over geological time (Mars). Too high: thick hydrogen envelope, no rocky surface (Neptune, Jupiter).

Planet Classes

  • Terrestrial (0.1–5 Earth masses)Iron core, silicate mantle, thin or secondary atmospheres, solid surfaces. Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars.
  • Super-Earths (2–10 Earth masses)Most common planet type found around other stars. May be rocky, water-world, or mini-Neptune. Composition is mass-dependent.
  • Ice Giants (10–20 Earth masses)Rock + water/ammonia/methane "ices" with thick hydrogen/helium envelope. Uranus, Neptune.
  • Gas Giants (>50 Earth masses)Dense possible core, massive H/He envelope, no solid surface. Runaway gas accretion during formation. Jupiter, Saturn.

Thermal & Geological Evolution

Planetary heat sources include accretional heating, radioactive decay, core formation, and tidal heating. This internal heat dictates whether plate tectonics can operate and whether magnetic fields form. Earth's protective dynamo requires a liquid metallic core and sufficient mass to maintain convection — a combination that may be rare.

The Periodic Table

The Chemistry of Life

Earth's biological signatures rely on the delivery and processing of volatile elements: carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, and oxygen (CHNO). The foundation of life as we know it. [Bergin, 2020]

  • DNA & the Genetic CodeA double-helix polymer of nucleotides encoding the information required to build and operate all known life. The human genome contains ~3 billion base pairs.
  • The Miller-Urey ExperimentStanley Miller and Harold Urey demonstrated in 1953 that organic molecules — including amino acids — could be synthesized abiotically from simple inorganic compounds and energy, suggesting life's building blocks could arise spontaneously.
  • Cell DivisionLife perpetuates itself through mitosis and meiosis — the choreographed duplication and halving of genetic information across billions of generations.

Planet Earth:
Geology & Formation

How Earth Formed

  • Classical Planetesimal Accretion [Chambers, 2004]Dust grains in the early solar nebula clumped into mountain-sized planetesimals, which merged into Moon-to-Mars-sized planetary embryos over hundreds of thousands of years. The embryos then underwent chaotic giant impacts to form the terrestrial planets.
  • Pebble Accretion [Boyle, 2022]Observations of distant protoplanetary disks forced a revision of the classical model. Planets likely formed much faster, as millimeter-to-meter-sized pebbles drifted inward and were efficiently swept up by the gravity of growing planetary cores.

Earth's Climate Thermostat

Earth managed to maintain a habitable climate and liquid water over billions of years due to the carbonate-silicate geochemical cycle. [Kasting, Toon & Pollack, 1988]

This cycle acts as a planetary thermostat: atmospheric CO₂ is drawn down through weathering and locked in carbonate rocks, then released through volcanism. The negative feedback prevents Earth from experiencing runaway greenhouse warming (like Venus) or permanent glaciation (like Mars).

Biosignatures

Identifying life on other worlds requires analyzing a planet's atmospheric and surface chemistry for states of extreme disequilibrium. [Schwieterman & Leung, 2024]

On Earth, the simultaneous presence of highly reactive gases — abundant oxygen coexisting with methane — is a robust biosignature, because these gases would rapidly destroy each other without a continuous biological source replenishing them. Additionally, vegetation creates a "red edge" — a sharp increase in surface reflectance that could be detected by a distant telescope. [Seager, 2009]

Key Events in Evolutionary History

  • Early Marine Explosion [Kröger et al., 2011]The fossil record demonstrates dramatic evolutionary radiations long before modern ecosystems stabilized — including the emergence of complex cephalopods in the oceans of the Ordovician.
  • Mammals Among Dinosaurs [Luo, 2007]Early mammals underwent rapid transformation, occupying highly diverse ecological niches during the age of dinosaurs — not after it.
  • The Human Bottleneck [Hu et al., 2023]Genomic evidence reveals that human ancestors faced a near-extinction population bottleneck roughly 900,000 years ago during the Early to Middle Pleistocene transition, severely restricting and shaping modern genetic diversity.
  • The Most Invasive Species [Marean, 2015]Homo sapiens dominated the globe through a unique evolutionary combination of "hyper-prosociality" — extensive cooperation with non-kin — and advanced projectile weapons, allowing us to outcompete Neanderthals and all other hominids.
  • The Great Filter Framework [Bostrom, 2008]Earth's evolutionary history can be framed as having successfully passed through a series of "Great Filters" — highly improbable evolutionary leaps like the emergence of eukaryotic cells and advanced intelligence — which may explain why we appear to be alone.

The AI Paradox

  • AI Expands Individual Impact but Narrows Collective Focus [Hao et al., forthcoming, Nature]AI tools dramatically increase the output and impact of individual scientists — but simultaneously restrict the collective focus of entire scientific fields. AI-augmented research gravitates toward established, data-rich areas, automating existing paradigms rather than exploring truly novel ones.
  • Human-Aware AI Systems [Sourati & Evans, 2023, Nature Human Behaviour]For AI to meaningfully accelerate discovery, it must be designed as "human-aware" — systems that understand human hypotheses, target our collective blind spots, and suggest the novel, unexplored connections we naturally miss.
  • The Benefit of Bias [Lai et al., 2025]Perfectly neutral AI systems can be detrimental by inducing "automation bias" — causing humans to uncritically accept AI output. Introducing culturally or politically biased AI actually improves collective human decision-making by provoking skepticism and critical engagement.

Communication Technology

We now carry devices in our pockets that connect us to every other human on Earth in real time. This mass communication infrastructure — phones, the internet, radio — transformed everything about how knowledge moves, how culture forms, and how movements organize. We include both the fact of this technology and its social implications.

For interstellar communication specifically: narrow-band radio transmissions remain the most efficient known method. The search for radio "technosignatures" defines the practical limits of information transmission at cosmic scales. [Margot et al., 2019]

What We Still
Don't Know

Honesty about the limits of our understanding may be the most important thing we can communicate.

  • Dark MatterAbout 27% of the universe's energy content is in a form we cannot see, touch, or detect directly. We know it exists because of its gravitational effects on galaxies and galaxy clusters. We have no confirmed particle candidate.
  • Dark EnergyAbout 68% of the universe's energy content drives accelerated expansion — a constant vacuum energy of empty space. We have no physical theory that predicts its observed value. It is perhaps the largest unsolved problem in physics.
  • ConsciousnessThe "hard problem": how do objective, physical neural networks produce subjective experience? Why is there something it is like to be a mind? We have no satisfying theory.
  • Fusion PowerStars have been fusing hydrogen into helium for 13 billion years. We have been trying to do it sustainably on Earth for 70 years. Controlled fusion would provide essentially unlimited clean energy.
  • The Origin of LifeHow did the first self-replicating molecule arise from chemistry? The Miller-Urey experiment showed the pieces can exist — but the assembly remains unknown.
  • The Fermi ParadoxIf the Drake Equation suggests many civilizations should exist, where is everybody? The silence of the cosmos is either evidence that we are rare, that civilizations don't last, or that we are not listening correctly.
